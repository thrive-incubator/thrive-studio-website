The thinking behind the Studio — the workforce thesis, what the pipeline is teaching us, and honest post-mortems on what we killed. Most studios publish their wins. We publish the kills too, because that's the part you can actually learn from.

Published · post-mortem

We killed Thrive Compass. Here's the whole story.

Thrive Compass was a standalone platform for community health workers — the kind of frontline workforce the Studio is built to equip. It had real internal energy behind it. We held it, then we shut it down. This is what happened and why, written without the gloss.

What we set out to build

A standalone platform for community health workers — software meant to equip CHWs for the mental health work they already do in the field. On paper it sat dead center in our thesis: the leverage is the adults already in the room, not more clinicians, and CHWs are exactly that workforce. The internal conviction was high. So was ours, for a while.

The honest version is that the idea passed the pre-filter on mission fit and enthusiasm, and we wanted it to be true. That's a normal place for an idea to start. It is not a reason to ship.

What the buyer signal actually said

Thin and contradictory. We invest upstream in validation precisely so the bet gets made before there's a company — desk research, customer discovery, a search for a real buyer who would pay. When we went looking for that buyer, the answer kept moving. Everyone agreed CHWs mattered. Nobody we talked to could tell us, consistently, who would write the check, for what, on what budget line. Enthusiasm was high; the data underneath it wasn't.

Our second operating principle is data over enthusiasm. Letters of intent, signed commitments, paid pilots — those are the test, and founder conviction is not. Compass never produced that signal. It produced a lot of nodding.

Why we killed it

Two principles did the work. Kill fast: kill criteria are set at the start of each stage, so when the buyer signal stayed contradictory we stopped rather than working through it on hope. Progressive commitment: we commit to doing the next stage's work, never to shipping the venture — which meant walking away cost us validation time, not a launched company we'd have to unwind.

We say no a lot. That's the point. The four-stage gate doesn't exist to ship every idea; it exists to be honest about which ones shouldn't ship. Compass is one of the ones that shouldn't have, and the gate said so before we'd spent a year proving it the expensive way.

What we kept

The thesis, intact. The diagnosis behind Compass — that the frontline workforce around the child is the leverage point — is exactly right, and it's still where every venture in the pipeline lives. What was wrong was the shape of the bet: a standalone CHW platform with no durable buyer underneath it. The reframing survives even when a specific product doesn't.

So the workforce thesis didn't die with Compass; it moved. Attunify is defining the competency standard for the consultation workforce and is already enrolling. Carepath DSP is equipping Direct Service Providers. The learning from the kill recycled into how we read buyer signal on the next idea — which is the entire reason we write the post-mortem down instead of quietly deleting the row.
