For operators

You've done the hardest part once.

Do it again inside an institution that de-risks the rest. You don't start with a cold deck and a room of skeptical VCs — you start with a validated idea, Georgetown's brand, regulatory tailwind, and a path to a profitable company. No unicorns required.

Who this is for

A "yes" tends to look like all four of these.

01

Mid-career & financially stable

You've been around long enough to understand reality. You don't need this to survive — that pressure distorts every decision an operator makes, and we'd rather you join from solid ground.

02

Built or operated something real

You shipped a product and saw it through, or you held P&L for a meaningful operating unit. Either one teaches the same thing: what it actually costs to ship.

03

Comfortable selling to systems

B2B or B2G shaped. You're at home with long sales cycles, procurement, RFPs, and pilot-to-contract motions. You're not trying to build a $9.99/mo app.

04

At home with sustainable scale

A $3–15M ARR profitable business, built lean on AI, is a win in your book — not a consolation prize on the way to a unicorn.

If you can only get out of bed for a billion-dollar exit, you'll be miserable here — and we'd rather say so now than three months in.

What's waiting on arrival

You join further down the field than a cold start.

Most operators start a company with a deck and a hypothesis. Here, much of the early risk is already retired before you walk in — and what's left is the part you're actually good at.

  • A validated problem. Desk research, customer discovery, and an MVP can already be in hand before you arrive. The bet is made before there's a company — you're staffing a venture the Studio already believes in, not pitching a cold idea.
  • Georgetown's weight. The brand, research access, and a domain network in early childhood, behavioral health, and family services — anchored in a SAMHSA-designated National Center of Excellence with roughly thirty years of applied research behind it.
  • A real proof point. Attunify, the Studio's first spin-out, opened its founding cohort in June 2026 — 267 enrolled professionals, ~$100K in first-cohort revenue, $0 VC raised. Not theoretical; already generating revenue.
  • The no-unicorn model. A path to a profitable company that doesn't depend on a moonshot — ~80% portfolio survival target, modest 3–5x multiples, capital that recycles, and mission-aligned governance.
Two ways in

Come see what we're building before you decide to jump in.

Two entry paths, and they're not mutually exclusive. The Advisory Council is a lighter-weight gateway that converts into a full EIR engagement when the right idea appears.

Lighter touch
Advisory Council
~2 hours a week, a one-year initial commitment, direct access to Studio projects, and a public Georgetown affiliation — "Advisor, Thrive Studio at Georgetown." A relationship-building mechanism, not a consulting gig: come see the work before you decide to run something.
Full engagement
Entrepreneur in Residence
Lead a validated idea through prototyping, MVP, and spin-out under an explicit EIR service + equity agreement. Meaningful time and real ownership of the venture, with the Studio's resources behind you. This is the path for operators ready to run.
To be clear

What Thrive Studio is not.

Saying what we're not is how an operator self-selects in or out. If any of these is a deal-breaker, better to know now.

  • Not a VC fund. Different portfolio math — ~80% survival, not one-in-twenty carry. Different time horizon, different relationship with the mission.
  • Not an accelerator. We don't run a cohort of external founders pitching their own startups. Ideas are brought by clinicians and researchers and developed inside the Studio; operators are paired with validated ones.
  • Not the Thrive Innovation Hub. That's a separate Center program — a fellowship for external startups. Easy to confuse on first contact; worth correcting early.
  • Not B2C or consumer mental health. The point of impact is the workforce around the child, not the consumer. Consumer unit economics work against the populations the thesis is built around.
  • Not a clean break post-spin-out. Thrive keeps an equity stake, a board seat, and a brand link. The shape is closer to a coalition than a transaction — operators who want a clean exit are the wrong fit.
