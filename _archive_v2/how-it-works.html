How we work

A real go/no-go at every gate. No cohorts. No demo day.

We run as a portfolio manager, not a judge. An idea passes a five-question pre-filter, then moves through four gated stages toward one of three honest outcomes. Most ideas don't advance through every stage — by design, not by accident.

What we are

Unambiguously a studio. Not an accelerator. Not an incubator.

We're not an accelerator running cohorts of external founders pitching their own startups, and we're not an incubator handing out desk space and mentorship. We generate and validate the ideas ourselves, supply the operator, the capital, and the infrastructure, and take a co-founder-level stake. The three words get used loosely in the field; here's the line we hold.

Incubator
Nurtures the founder's own idea
Open-ended support for idea-stage founders — office space, mentorship, small or no capital. The founder owns the idea and brings it in.
Accelerator
A fixed cohort with traction
A 3–6 month cohort for teams that already have an MVP. A small check for roughly 5–10% equity, ending in a demo day (YC, Techstars).
Venture studio · us
Builds the company from scratch
We generate and validate ideas internally, supply the founding operator plus capital and infrastructure, and take a co-founder-level stake. No cohort, no demo day.
Before anything

The pre-filter. Five questions, before any team time is spent.

  • Mission fit. Does it advance children's wellbeing in line with Thrive's mission?
  • A business, not a grant. Is there a plausible business model — not just a good grant idea?
  • Real buyers. Is there basic reason to believe paying customers exist?
  • One sentence. Can the hypothesis be stated in a single sentence?
  • A willing leader. Is someone ready to lead it with conviction?
The four gated stages

Progressive commitment. We commit to the next stage, not to the outcome.

01

Committee Review

The idea goes to the three-person Gate Committee — Matt, Jason, and JB — for an up-or-down call on whether to invest desk-research time. Kill criteria are named here, at the start, not improvised later.

DeliverableOne-page idea brief
Operator's roleMay surface or sponsor the idea
GateInvest research time — yes or no
02

Desk Research

Roughly two weeks at six to twelve hours per week: market sizing, competitive landscape, feasibility. We run one active desk-research project at a time — concentration over dilution — then return to the committee.

DeliverableMarket + feasibility memo
Operator's roleOften paired here as the idea sharpens
GateAdvance to prototyping, or exit
03

Prototyping & Customer Discovery

A six-to-eight-week phase budgeted at about fifty hours of customer development and forty hours of prototyping. It ends on a real-buyer-signal review — letters of intent, paid pilots, signed commitments. Data over enthusiasm.

DeliverableDiscovery package + prototype
Operator's roleLeads discovery and the build
GateReal buyer signal, or exit
04

Deep Validation

Full MVP, detailed cost model, team confirmation, and governance design — leading to the final call. By here the operator is running the venture, and the only question is which of the three outcomes it's actually on.

DeliverableFull validation + spin-out structure
Operator's roleRuns the venture
GateSpin out · spin in · shut down
Three outcomes

No default outcome. The point is to be honest about which path each venture is on.

Spin out
An independent company
Incorporated and launched. Thrive retains an equity stake, a board seat, the brand link, and a cost-recovery relationship. Closer to a coalition than a clean break.
Spin in
A durable Center program
The venture stays inside the Thrive Center as a sustainable internal product line rather than a separate company.
Shut down
A clean post-mortem
Stopped with documented learnings. An early exit saves the operator and the field months of misdirected effort. Exits are celebrated alongside advances.

We say no a lot. That's the point.

Built not to fail

Venture studios fail for documented reasons. We engineered against each one.

Lack of focus. Industry-agnostic studios chase whatever looks hot.
→
A tight workforce / IECMHC niche and a written thesis. Off-thesis ideas get rejected, not entertained.
Launching too many ventures. Spreading thin until nothing gets real attention.
→
A hard cap on concurrent builds and one active desk-research project at a time. Two spin-outs a year is a ceiling, not a quota.
Insufficient kill discipline. Keeping zombie ventures alive on sunk cost.
→
Pre-committed kill criteria at every gate. We say no a lot — and we publish the post-mortems.
Follow-on "dead equity." A messy cap table that scares off later investors.
→
A non-VC model with a clean cap structure. We aren't stacking growth-stage rounds on top of a studio stake.
Funding as annual OpEx. A studio on the yearly chopping block can't commit.
→
A multi-year balance-sheet and philanthropic funding base, so the Studio isn't re-justifying its existence every twelve months.
Misaligned founder incentives. The "phantom founder" who never really owned the outcome.
→
An operator recruited and paired with an explicit service and equity agreement — incentives written down, not assumed.
Identity-less "startup factory." A nameless machine no one trusts or remembers.
→
A strong Center brand and mission, with the brand link retained after spin-out. The institution is the identity.
